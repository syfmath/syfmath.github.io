1. Vortex Equation

We consider the vortex equation:

\begin{cases}
dx(u, t) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||x(u, t) - y|| , \mu_t(dy) , dt, \
x(u, 0) = u, , u \in \mathbb{R}^2, \
\mu_t = \mu_0 \circ x(\cdot, t)^{-1}, , t \geq 0.
\end{cases}

This equation has two invariants:

\begin{cases}
I_1 = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} u , \mu_t(du) \equiv \text{Const}, \quad I_2 = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} ||u - m||^2 , \mu_t(du) \equiv \text{Const}
\end{cases}

where \begin{cases} m = I_1(t) \end{cases} represents the center of mass.

Proof of Invariants

  • For ( I_1 ):

    By symmetry in ( u, v ), we have:

    \begin{cases}
    \frac{d}{dt} I_1 = \frac{d}{dt} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} u , \mu_t(du) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||x(u, t) - x(v, t)|| , \mu_0(dv) , \mu_0(du) = 0
    \end{cases}

  • For ( I_2 ):

    Setting ( \tilde{x}(u, t) = x(u, t) - m ), and using the symmetry of ( u ) and ( v ), we obtain:

    \begin{cases}
    \frac{d}{dt} I_2 = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \tilde{x}(u, t) \cdot \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||\tilde{x}(u, t) - \tilde{x}(v, t)|| , \mu_0(dv) , \mu_0(du) = 0
    \end{cases}

2. Differential Equation for ( \mu_t )

For ( \varphi \in D(\mathbb{R}^2) = C_0^\infty(\mathbb{R}^2) ), we derive:

\begin{cases}
\frac{d}{dt} \langle \mu_t, \varphi \rangle = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \left\langle \nabla \varphi(u), \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||u - v|| , \mu_t(dv) \right\rangle \mu_t(du) , dt
\end{cases}

Using integration by parts and Stokes’ theorem:

\begin{cases}
d\mu_t = \left[ \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \text{div} \frac{(u - v)^\perp}{||u - v||^2} , \mu_t(dv) , \mu_t - \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||u - v|| , \mu_t(dv) , \nabla \mu_t \right] dt
\end{cases}

Assuming ( \frac{d\mu_0}{d\lambda^2} = \rho_0 ), we derive the partial differential equation for ( \rho_t ):

\begin{cases}
\rho_t = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||u - v|| , \mu_t(dv) , \nabla \rho_t, \
\rho_0 = \rho(0)
\end{cases}

Behavior of the Equation

Considering:

\begin{cases}
d\tilde{\mu}t = -\int{\mathbb{R}^2} \nabla^\perp \ln ||u - v|| , \tilde{\mu}_t(dv) , \nabla \tilde{\mu}_t , dt + \Delta \tilde{\mu}_t , dt
\end{cases}

3. Heat Equation in ( \mathbb{R}^1 \times [0, +\infty) )

If we have the heat equation:

\begin{cases}
\frac{\partial \rho(x, t)}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \rho(x, t),
\end{cases}

then

\begin{cases}
\rho(x, t) = \rho(x, 0) * p_t, \quad p_t \sim N(0, t),
\end{cases}

with:

\begin{cases}
\rho(x, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi t}} \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-\frac{(x - y)^2}{2t}} , \rho(y, 0) , dy
\end{cases}

4. Problems

Problem 1

Consider the vortex equation with mass distribution ( +\Delta \mu_t ). Explore the limits as ( t \to +\infty ). At this time, ( I_1(t) ) and ( I_2(t) ) vary with ( t ). Determine their rate of change and limits.

Solution Strategy

  1. Focus on the Laplacian term:

    For ( I_1(t) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} u , d\mu_t ):

    \begin{cases}
    \frac{dI_1(t)}{dt} = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} u , \Delta \rho_t(u) , du = 0
    \end{cases}

    implying ( m(t) = I_1(t) \equiv C ).

  2. For ( I_2(t) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} ||u - m(t)||^2 , \mu_t(du) ):

    \begin{cases}
    \frac{dI_2(t)}{dt} = 2
    \end{cases}

    Thus, ( I_2(t) = 2t + I_2(0) ).

Problem 2

Find a suitable normalization to make ( I_2(t) ) constant under the diffusion term.

Solution Strategy

Using the measure ( \mu_t(t^s du) ) for ( s \neq 0 ):

\begin{cases}
dI_2(t) = 2st^{-s-1}
\end{cases}

Therefore, this normalization fails to keep ( I_2(t) ) constant regardless of ( s ).
